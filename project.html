Out of Site, Out of Mind

An exploration of the plastic waste trade

Project aims

This project seeks to understand the plastic waste trade and plastic pollution through the lens of waste colonialism. The term 'waste colonialism' refers to the practice of exporting waste from high income to low income countries. The key question I want to ask is, how much of our plastic waste ends up in the ocean?

Following China's plastic import ban in 2020, Malaysia and several other developing countries, such as Vietnam and Turkey, became the top destination for plastic waste from developed countries. At the same time, mainstream media frequently blames these countries for being the world's large polluters of plastic waste, when these are actually outsourced from developed nations.

Data sources:

Given increasing global concerns regarding plastic pollution, I leveraged various sources to obtain the necessary data for my visualisation project. Using the programming skills acquired in PP434, such as Python and Vegalite, I was able to access and clean large datasets relevant to plastic waste.

The first approach involved automated data extraction, where I used APIs from Our World in Data (OWID) and the World Bank (TWB). I supplemented the first approach by manually downloading other relevant data related to plastic waste from UN Comtrade, UN Trade and Development (UNCTAD), UNEP's Global Partnership on Plastic Pollution and Marine Litter (GPML), and the National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI).

Challenges

    One of the key issues I encountered during the project was when I attempted to use the UN Comtrade API in Python, despite the API being publicly available and retrieving my subscription key. To overcome this, I manually downloaded the data required in CSV format through the UN Comtrade’s website and filtered the relevant data for my project. The API for other websites, such as UNCTAD, was only available in R, while the other data sources did not have API. Thus, I had to manually download, clean, and process these datasets in Python.

    Despite the large amount of data on plastic waste, most of these datasets were inconsistent and I encountered a lot of missing values, such as ISO3 codes for some of the datasets. I turned to Python libraries to address these challenges, such as by using pandas and py country to conduct data manipulation and analysis.

    Furthermore, there was also limited data on plastic waste for smaller and low-income countries, which may not accurately reflect the plastic pollution issue in these respective countries.

Conclusion

    Globally, plastic waste consumption is increasing and will continue to increase in the foreseeable future, as illustrated in Section 1. With increasing consumption, trade in plastic waste will increase, resulting in irreversible implications for the environment and wider society.

    As portrayed by the visualisations in Sections 2 – 4, there is also some element of waste colonialism in the plastic waste trade. Top exporters of plastic waste are mostly high-income countries, while the importing countries are middle-income countries that may lack the proper infrastructure to recycle or sustainably dispose of plastic waste. Thus, they effectively become a dumping site for richer countries. This calls for further work to examine the destinations of plastic waste from top exporters.

    The amount of plastic waste emitted into the ocean, which later disintegrates into microplastics, is an alarming phenomenon with wide-reaching environmental and health implications. Policy intervention is therefore necessary to address the root causes of plastic pollution, with a specific focus on equitable solutions that promote environmental justice.
